Creative Direction

Editorial, not documentary.
Sensory, not sterile.

LUCCA's content should feel alive — you should hear the sizzle, smell the rosemary, want to reach into the screen. We're making food cinema, not menu photos.

✦ The LUCCA Standard

  • Shallow depth of field — draw the eye to texture, juice, steam
  • Warm directional light — side windows, kitchen flames, candles
  • Slow motion culinary sequences — cutting, pouring, plating
  • Clean, modern composition — white space, negative space, minimalist
  • Sound design — sizzle, knife cuts, wine pour, ambient chatter
  • Human hands — the chef's touch, the sommelier's pour

✕ What We Avoid

  • Overhead iPhone shots with flash — instant amateur energy
  • Flat fluorescent lighting — kills appetite and ambiance
  • Over-saturated colors — Instagram filter energy
  • Generic stock-looking interiors with no soul
  • Forced smiles and staged "happy customer" poses
  • Music that sounds like a food truck commercial
Color Science

The LUCCA Grade

Warm, appetizing, modern. Golden highlights with clean whites and rich shadows. The grade should make every dish look irresistible and every space feel like a destination.

Ivory #FAFAF7
Cream #F5F0EB
Gold #C8A960
Seared #8B4513
Charcoal #2C2C2C
Espresso #0D0D0B

Shadows

Rich and warm, never cold or blue. Deep espresso blacks with subtle amber undertones. Shadows should feel like the corner of a candlelit dining room.

Food Tones

Protected and appetizing. Reds stay rich (steak), greens stay vibrant (herbs), and golden-brown crust pops. Never desaturate food. Ever.

Highlights

Clean ivory roll-off on marble and white plates. Warm golden halation on candle flames and pendant lights. Never blue or clinical.

Ambient Light

Use the venue's own lighting as a feature. Pendant glow, kitchen flame spill, window light. Support it, don't fight it. Mixed temps = atmosphere.

Production Schedule

Two days. Every angle covered.

Day one for food, kitchen, and interior content. Day two for service, atmosphere, and team portraits. Structured for zero interruption to operations.

Day One
Full Day — Kitchen + Food Photography (~10 hours)
8:00 – 9:00 AM
Arrive + Scout
Lighting assessment, interior walkthrough, set up food photography station with controlled lighting. Mark hero angles for kitchen coverage.
9:00 AM – 12:00 PM
Food Photography — Signature Dishes
Controlled studio-style food photography. Each hero dish individually plated and shot — overhead, 45°, and macro. Focus stacking for menu prints. Chef works alongside camera team.
12:00 – 1:00 PM
Break + Card Swap
Offload media. Review hero shots with chef/owner. Charge batteries for afternoon kitchen session.
1:00 – 4:00 PM
Kitchen Cinema — Live Cooking
Cinematic A-cam and B-cam capture of full cook sequences: searing, basting, plating, knife work. Macro inserts at 120fps for slow-motion sizzle and pour shots. Sound recorded separately for mix.
4:00 – 6:00 PM
Interior + Detail B-Roll
Empty restaurant beauty shots while light is perfect: marble bar, glassware, table settings, pendant lights, menu details, signage. Gimbal walkthrough from entrance to private dining.
Day Two
Evening Session — Service + Atmosphere (~6 hours)
3:00 – 4:30 PM
Team Portraits + Bar Content
Editorial portraits of chef, sous chef, sommelier, bartender, and management. Bar program content — cocktail builds, wine pulling, garnish prep.
4:30 – 5:30 PM
Pre-Service Ritual
The calm before the storm. Staff lineup, mise en place, final polish on glassware, napkin folds. The moments that show the discipline behind the luxury.
5:30 – 9:00 PM
Live Service — Full Atmosphere
The restaurant in motion. Guests arriving, food hitting tables, glasses clinking, candlelight reflections, sommelier presentations. Two cameras capturing the energy of a full house. The content that makes people want to be there.
